Case 01 · Scalable Authoring Systems

Replacing bespoke content creation with a scalable authoring system

Built a developer-facing content pipeline that exposed a previously gated workflow to a broader team, defining a shared library of reusable visual and behavior components (“atoms”) and a consistent authoring model across disciplines, allowing previously expensive and complex content to be efficiently authored without hard cross-team dependencies.

+

Problem

Boss and encounter development was bespoke and highly coupled across multiple teams in active production. A small group of combatant designers owned the workflow, limiting scale and creating repeated implementation work across content. Each new piece of content required rebuilding behavior, logic, and structure from scratch, creating slow iteration, inconsistent quality, high integration risk, and limited ability for downstream teams to contribute effectively.

Constraints

  • Ongoing production with active content demands.
  • Multiple teams contributing to encounters and combatants.
  • Limited engineering bandwidth for new systems.
  • Need for reliability under high-stakes live conditions.

Approach

  • Exposed an internal workflow to a broader group of designers through a developer-facing content pipeline.
  • Established reusable visual and behavior components (“atoms”) that could be composed into complex encounters.
  • Defined shared conventions across disciplines so visual, gameplay, and design work integrated cleanly within the same system.
  • Constrained the system so non-specialists could safely prototype content without deep system knowledge.
  • Ensured new requests folded back into the shared library, accumulating value instead of creating one-off solutions.
  • Demonstrated system viability through low-cost implementations to secure buy-in before full investment.

Key Insight

Systems should accumulate value over time, not reset with each use. Every new piece of content should strengthen the system, not bypass it.

Outcome

  • Expanded complex encounter creation from a small group of combatant designers to encounter designers and downstream contributors.
  • Moved iteration from centralized implementation into local authoring loops, reducing cross-team handoffs.
  • Replaced repeated bespoke implementations with a shared library of gameplay and visual components.
  • Reduced implementation variance across designers and teams.
  • Improved reliability and predictability while creating a system that compounds value through built-in reuse.
Case 02 · Consolidation + Tooling Recovery

Collapsing redundant systems into a single set of supported features and workflows

Collapsed competing systems into a single supported path, restoring authoring velocity and reducing ship risk.

+

Problem

Multiple systems existed with overlapping responsibility and unclear ownership. This created inconsistent behavior, confusion about which path to support, duplicated effort, and increased risk approaching ship. In parallel, authoring tools lacked reliable support for required repositioning logic.

Constraints

  • Late-stage production with active content in development.
  • Multiple teams already depending on existing workflows.
  • Limited engineering bandwidth for new tooling.
  • Need to stabilize behavior quickly without adding unnecessary risk.

Approach

  • Audited competing systems and deprecated redundant paths in favor of a viable shipping plan.
  • Communicated and enforced a single supported workflow across teams.
  • Identified authoring gaps, specifically around required repositioning logic.
  • Repurposed legacy hooks from prior systems into the current structure.
  • Built a minimal working fallback to prove viability and shift the conversation from opinion to evidence.
  • Aligned design and engineering around stable, extensible solutions for adjacent high-risk systems.

Key Insight

Ambiguity in systems is more costly than limitation in systems. A single, well-supported path enables progress faster than multiple competing ones.

Outcome

  • Eliminated redundant systems and aligned teams around a single supported workflow during late-stage production.
  • Reduced integration risk approaching ship by removing competing system paths.
  • Delivered owned system changes through ship without feature loss or rollback.
  • Restored required authoring capability without requiring full new tooling investment.
  • Replaced high-risk implementations with stable, production-ready components.
Case 03 · AI Behavior Systems

Establishing Predictable Behavior by Eliminating Special-Casing

Rebooted inherited AI behavior and perception systems to align with product needs under shifting product constraints and reduced team continuity, establishing a consistent cause-and-effect model that made behavior predictable, extensible, and reliable for ship.

+

Problem

The AI behavior and perception systems were inherited from prior game design constraints and did not fit the product that was actively being developed and shipping. Team attrition had removed original system authors, creating unclear ownership and making it difficult to extend behavior without introducing edge cases.

Constraints

  • Existing dependencies across gameplay, AI, and content.
  • Ongoing feature and tuning requirements during active production.
  • Team attrition removed original system authors and context.
  • Multiple disciplines relied on system behavior.
  • Reduced engineering support late in production.

Approach

  • Reconstructed and audited inherited systems without original authors, then refit them around current product needs.
  • Reduced feature scope early to establish a viable path to ship.
  • Identified perception as a systemic failure point and re-established it as a consistent cause-and-effect system.
  • Redefined behavior around clear inputs and outputs rather than special-case implementations.
  • Established constraints that ensured consistent behavior across scenarios.
  • Maintained system-level oversight through ownership transitions while delegating implementation.

Key Insight

Systems are often correct for the constraints they were built under, but those constraints change. Effective system design requires recognizing when prior assumptions no longer align with current product needs.

Outcome

  • Established a consistent and predictable AI behavior model.
  • Reduced reliance on special-case logic and lowered long-term complexity.
  • Enabled late-stage gameplay features to be implemented without introducing edge cases or additional system changes.
  • Improved alignment between gameplay intent and system behavior.
  • Delivered a complex system through multiple ownership transitions and cross-functional dependencies.
Case 04 · Contract-Based Interaction Workflows

Maintaining Authorship in AI-Assisted Development

Defined and enforced a contract-based interaction model to constrain AI-assisted workflows on a solo project, reducing ambiguity, preserving authorship, and enabling predictable iteration on non-deterministic systems.

+

Context

This work represents a shift from real-time systems design into AI-assisted workflows, applying contract-based thinking to non-deterministic systems during active development of an independent game project.

Problem

AI-assisted workflows can violate authorship when outputs expand beyond defined intent, reinterpret scope, or introduce unrequested alternatives. This creates competing solution paths, ambiguous system state, and validation cost that grows faster than the work itself.

Constraints

  • No direct control over model execution.
  • Interaction occurs through natural language only.
  • No persistent enforcement layer.
  • Development requires strict alignment between intent and change.
  • Assumes a single authoritative source of intent.

Approach

  • Established and enforced a strict interaction contract during AI-assisted development workflows.
  • Defined the user as the sole authority for intent and scope.
  • Constrained the model to execute requested operations only, using provided context only.
  • Disallowed speculation, alternate solutions, abstraction, redesign, and partial edits without full context.
  • Enforced single-path execution: one hypothesis, one change, stop after execution.
  • Required outputs to be directly verifiable against the supplied system context.

Key Insight

Without an execution contract, AI systems expand the state space uncontrollably. Constraining valid operations collapses the state space, enforces single-path execution, and preserves authorship.

Outcome

  • Maintained authorship across system changes.
  • Eliminated competing solution paths and speculative edits.
  • Reduced ambiguity and validation overhead during iteration.
  • Enabled deterministic workflows on top of a non-deterministic system.
  • Sustained a “latest build always playable” workflow throughout ongoing development.
  • Enabled safe system-level changes, including a spawn system rewrite, without breaking existing behavior or integration contracts.
  • Validated a core architecture that could evolve without breaking existing behavior.
