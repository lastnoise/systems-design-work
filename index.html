Case 01 · Scalable Authoring Systems

Designing a Scalable System for Complex Interactive Content

Transformed bespoke boss development into a reusable authoring system that enabled repeatable creation, downstream prototyping, and compounding value through built-in content reuse.

+

Problem

Boss and encounter development was bespoke and highly coupled. Each new piece of content required rebuilding behavior, logic, and structure from scratch, creating slow iteration, inconsistent quality, high integration risk, and limited ability for downstream teams to contribute effectively.

Constraints

  • Ongoing production with active content demands.
  • Multiple teams contributing to encounters and combatants.
  • Limited engineering bandwidth for new systems.
  • Need for reliability under high-stakes live conditions.

Approach

  • Transitioned boss development from bespoke implementations to a modular, system-driven approach.
  • Defined reusable behavior patterns instead of one-off implementations.
  • Established clear interaction contracts between systems.
  • Structured authoring around composable components rather than custom logic.
  • Designed reuse as a first-class property of the system.
  • Enabled downstream teams to compose and prototype content without requiring upstream system changes.
  • Built low-cost versions of the system to demonstrate viability and secure buy-in before full investment.

Key Insight

Systems should accumulate value over time, not reset with each use. Every new piece of content should strengthen the system, not bypass it.

Outcome

  • Enabled repeatable authoring of complex encounters.
  • Reduced implementation variance across designers and teams.
  • Improved reliability and predictability of system behavior.
  • Shifted content creation from system authors to downstream designers, reducing dependency on specialized roles.
  • Enabled teams to prototype and validate ideas independently, improving the quality and clarity of downstream requests.
  • Created a system that compounds value through built-in reuse.
Case 02 · Consolidation + Tooling Recovery

Eliminating Redundant Systems and Restoring Authoring Workflows

Evaluated competing systems, cut redundant paths, restored critical tooling capability, and aligned teams around stable workflows for ship.

+

Problem

Multiple systems existed with overlapping responsibility and unclear ownership. This created inconsistent behavior, confusion about which path to support, duplicated effort, and increased risk approaching ship. In parallel, authoring tools lacked reliable support for required repositioning logic.

Constraints

  • Late-stage production with active content in development.
  • Multiple teams already depending on existing workflows.
  • Limited engineering bandwidth for new tooling.
  • Need to stabilize behavior quickly without adding unnecessary risk.

Approach

  • Audited competing implementations to determine capability, reliability, and integration cost.
  • Made a clear decision to deprecate one system and fully support the other.
  • Communicated the decision across teams and reinforced a single path forward.
  • Identified gaps in current authoring tools, specifically around repositioning logic.
  • Unearthed and repurposed legacy hooks from prior systems into the current structure.
  • Built a minimal working fallback to demonstrate tool viability and shift the conversation from opinion to proof.
  • Evaluated adjacent system implementations, identified high-risk approaches, and aligned design and engineering on stable, extensible solutions.

Key Insight

Ambiguity in systems is more costly than limitation in systems. A single, well-supported path enables progress faster than multiple competing ones.

Outcome

  • Eliminated system redundancy and aligned teams around a single supported workflow.
  • Reduced confusion and implementation variance across content.
  • Enabled reliable repositioning logic without requiring full new tooling investment.
  • Restored and refined authoring capability for ship.
  • Replaced high-risk implementations with stable, production-ready components.
  • Aligned engineering investment toward reliability and performance at ship.
Case 03 · AI Behavior Systems

Establishing Predictable Behavior by Eliminating Special-Casing

Refit inherited AI behavior systems around true cause-and-effect rules, making behavior more predictable, easier to extend, and easier to align with intended product experience.

+

Problem

The AI behavior system was inherited from prior assumptions and did not fit the needs of the product. It relied heavily on special-case logic, creating inconsistent behavior, increasing complexity with each feature, and making it difficult to align gameplay intent with system output.

Constraints

  • Existing dependencies across gameplay, AI, and content.
  • Ongoing feature and tuning requirements during active production.
  • Multiple disciplines relying on system behavior.
  • Limited production support for technical coordination.

Approach

  • Refit the system around the needs of the product, transitioning from conditional overrides to a consistent cause-and-effect model.
  • Redefined behavior around clear inputs and outputs rather than special-case implementations.
  • Established constraints that ensured consistent behavior across scenarios.
  • Aligned high-level goals weekly to ensure system correctness still supported the desired player experience.
  • Delegated implementation while maintaining system-level oversight and continuity through ownership transitions.
  • Coordinated across designers and engineers to keep a high-tendril technical effort moving toward ship.

Key Insight

Systems should behave predictably under all conditions, not just the ones they were explicitly designed for.

Outcome

  • Established a consistent and predictable AI behavior model.
  • Reduced reliance on special-case logic and lowered long-term complexity.
  • Enabled new gameplay features (e.g., stealth behaviors and weapon modifiers) to be implemented without additional system changes.
  • Improved alignment between gameplay intent and system behavior.
  • Delivered a complex system through multiple ownership transitions and cross-functional dependencies.
Case 04 · Contract-Based Interaction Workflows

Maintaining Authorship in AI-Assisted Development

Converted AI interaction from open-ended generation into a constrained execution workflow that preserved intent, reduced ambiguity, and enabled reliable iteration.

+

Problem

AI-assisted workflows can violate authorship when outputs expand beyond defined intent, reinterpret scope, or introduce unrequested alternatives. This creates competing solution paths, ambiguous system state, and validation cost that grows faster than the work itself.

Constraints

  • No direct control over model execution.
  • Interaction occurs through natural language only.
  • No persistent enforcement layer.
  • Development requires strict alignment between intent and change.
  • Assumes a single authoritative source of intent; does not attempt to resolve competing inputs.

Approach

  • Defined the user as the sole authority for intent and scope.
  • Constrained the model to execute requested operations only, using provided context only.
  • Disallowed speculation, alternate solutions, abstraction, redesign, and partial edits without full context.
  • Enforced single-path execution: one hypothesis, one change, stop after execution.
  • Required outputs to be directly verifiable against the supplied system context.

Key Insight

Without an execution contract, AI systems expand the state space uncontrollably. Constraining valid operations collapses the state space, enforces single-path execution, and preserves authorship.

Outcome

  • Maintained authorship across system changes.
  • Eliminated competing solution paths and speculative edits.
  • Reduced ambiguity and validation overhead during iteration.
  • Enabled deterministic workflows on top of a non-deterministic system.
